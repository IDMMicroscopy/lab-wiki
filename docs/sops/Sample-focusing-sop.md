## Purpose

This SOP provides step-by-step instructions for safely and effectively focusing samples at a light microscope to achieve optimal image quality while preventing damage to equipment and samples.

## Scope

This procedure applies to all users operating inverted light microscopes with various sample types including glass slides with coverslips, 35mm dishes, and multi-well plates.

## Safety

***Critical***: Never allow objectives to contact the sample or holder surface (slide, coverslip, dish, etc.) directly 
    

- Always maintain visual monitoring of distance between objective and sample during focusing 
    

- Improper focusing can result in: 
    

- Objective damage 
    

- Sample holder or stage damage 
    

- Sample breakage and leakage 
    

- Sample contamination of microscope stand or stage 
    

- Glass contamination of microscope stand or stage 
    

- Complete loss of sample

## Materials & equipment

- Inverted light microscope 
    

- Transmitted light source and/or fluorescence capabilities 
    

- Appropriate objectives (air or immersion) 
    

- Immersion media (oil, water, or silicone, as required) 
    

- Sample in appropriate container/format 
    

**NOTE ON SAMPLE SUITABILITY:** Always plan to image as much in the central region of your sample and holder as possible. This is the ensure your imaging region and stage travel during imaging does not bring the edge of the stage insert and stage close to or in contact with the objective. This is especially import with higher magnification and immersion objectives. Such contact can result in severe damge to the stage insert, stage, and/ or objective lens. 

- If using coverslips mounted on slides:  
    

- Mount only 1 coverslip per slide. Mount the coverslip and sample is close to the central portion of the slide (the full slide, not only the unfrosted region, if using frosted slides).  
    

- Ensure there is a margin of at least 2 mm (some stage inserts may require more) between the coverslip and the edge of the slide, to ensure the slide sits level in the stage insert 
    

- If using multi-well plates: Depending on the microscope and imaging objective used, you may not be able to image samples in the outmost wells (1-2 rows and columns, in a 96 well-plate).

## Procedure

1. **Initial Setup** 

	**1.1. System Initialization:**
- Verify the appropriate stage adapter is installed for your sample format and imaging plan 
    

- Verify microscope and software has initialized properly 
    

- Verify lowest available magnification objective is in the active position 
    

- Verify the objectives to be sued are clean and undamaged 
    

- Confirm stage has calibrated according to system requirements 
    

- Check all systems are functioning normally 
    

	**1.2. Objective Selection:** 
	
- Start with the lowest magnification objective you will need for your imaging. 

	**1.2.1. Objective Selection Guidelines** 
	

- For Large Field of View Analysis – i.e. tissue and cellular-scale imaging: 
    

- Use: 20x or 40X air objective maximum 
    

- Application: Cell-level or higher structure visualization, marker distributions 
    

- Note: Refractive index matching is not critical for image quality at this magnification and NA 
    

- For Subcellular Analysis: 
    

- Use: 40x, 63x, or 100x objectives 
    

- Types: Air or immersion objectives available 
    

- Critical: Refractive index matching becomes important at higher magnifications and NA (>/ 1.0) 
    

- Immersion Media: Water, silicone, or oil depending on objective specifications 
    

	**1.3. Objective Position:** 

- Ensure the objective is in its lowest position (furthest from the stage).  

- When changing samples during an active imaging session, it is sufficient to lower the objective to the “load” position 
    

2. **Immersion Objective Preparation (If Applicable)** 

***NB: Do not apply any immersion media to air objectives. Always apply to appropriate media to the lens, and per the objective specifications*** 

NB: This process applies to Inverted Microscopes: 

- Collect a small droplet (~15-20 µl) of immersion media on the lower edge of the applicator paddle or dropper 
    

- Transfer the droplet directly onto the objective using the applicator paddle 
    

- DO NOT touch the lens front with the applicator. Ensure the media droplet is transferred by surface tension only 
    

- Ensure the transferred amount is not too much such that it starts to run off the lens front 
    

3. **Sample Loading** 

	**3.1. Sample loading:** 
	Load sample (glass slide with coverslip, 35mm dish, or multi-well plate) into the sample holder in the stage adapter 

	**3.2. Sample & Stage Positioning:** 

	**3.2.1 Sample positioning:** 

- Ensure sample is level and stable on the stage 
    

- Sample must not be able to move or wobble when properly loaded in position 
    

- For slides and round imaging dishes, as small (0.5 mm ) range of freedom of lateral movement of the sample in the holder is fine and normal. 
    

- Sample should NOT be locked rigidly in position with holding brackets 
    

- Sample should be loosely but securely held: it should not be able to wobble and not be at risk of falling through the holding brackets, but if it were pushed from the bottom, it should be able to rise out of position 
    

- This is to prevent sample and objective damage in the case of erroneous focusing far above the focal plane 
    

- Position sample (sample area of slide or imaging dish) and brackets in approximately the central stage insert area) 
    

	**3.2.2 Stage Adjustment:** 
Adjust the microscope stage position as needed to align the objective with the desired sample area, so that the objective is directly beneath the area of interest for imaging (i.e where your sample is on the slide, or the plate well you plan to start imaging in, etc)  

- Aim to have the stage in approximately the centre of its travel range and the sample area of interest in the central region of the stage insert. 
    

5. **Focusing Procedure** 

	**5.1. Initial Approach (Air Objectives)** 

- Slowly raise objective toward sample using **macro focus knob** 

- Maintain visual contact with objective and sample 

- Stop when objective is approximately ~5mm from sample 

	**5.2. Fine Focusing (Air Objectives)** 

- Alternate rapidly (less than 1-second intervals) between: 

  - Looking directly at objective and sample 

  - Looking through microscope oculars/eyepieces 

- Continue raising objective slowly using **macro focus knob** initially 

- Switch to **fine focus knob** when approaching focus 

**Transmitted Light Indicators:** 

- Shadows and blurry shapes appear as focus approaches 

- Contrast and clarity increase until crisp focus achieved 

**Fluorescence Indicators:** 

- Background signal increases as uniform haze (e.g., blue with DAPI) 

- Haze resolves into discrete structures (e.g., nuclei) at proper focus 

	**5.3. Immersion Objective Focusing** 

- Raise objective while maintaining visual line of sight with objective and sample  

- Slow down the closer the objective gets to the sample 

- Continue just until immersion media contacts sample surface 

-  **Visual Indicator: Liquid will spread by surface tension, visible as change in light quality on sample** 

- Use **fine focus knob only** from this point 

- Maintain rapid alternation between direct visual inspection and ocular viewing 

- Slowly raise objective until sample is in focus 

-STOP before the objective directly contacts the sample and starts pushing it upwards 

If focus cannot easily be found: 

 Ensure objective is aligned with appropriate sample region 

Lower objective until immersion media contact is *just* lost, the repeat the focusing process with the fine focus knob 

6. **Critical Safety Points** 

	**6.1. Visual Monitoring** 

- **NEVER** lose visual contact with objective and sample during focusing 

- **ALWAYS** alternate between direct observation and ocular viewing 

- This prevents objective contact with sample 

	**6.2. Focus Limits** 

- **DO NOT** go past focal point 

- **DO NOT** allow objective to contact sample surface 

- **DO NOT** push sample upward on stage 

- **DO NOT** raise objective into stage or stage insert 

	**6.3. Consequences of Improper Focusing** 

- Objective damage (expensive repair/replacement) 

- Sample destruction and leakage 

- Potential injury from broken glass 

- Microscope contamination 

- Complete sample loss

## Troubleshooting

| Issue                      | Likely cause             | Fix                             |
| -------------------------- | ------------------------ | ------------------------------- |
| Cannot achieve focus       | Sample too thick         | Check sample preparation        |
| Blurry image               | Wrong immersion media    | Verify objective specifications |
| Oil bubbles in image       | Air in immersion oil     | Remove and reapply oil          |
| Sample moving during focus | Improper sample securing | Adjust sample holder            |
